# A University Research Partnership for Professor‑Led Quantitative Innovation

## Introduction

Universities are home to world-class expertise in mathematics, statistics, optimization, computational modeling, and other STEM-related disciplines—skills that form the foundation of quantitative finance. Faculty across these fields routinely train the researchers who go on to build some of the most successful systematic strategies in the industry. Yet the research groups that cultivate this talent often depend on limited and episodic funding.

This partnership offers a new path. During sabbaticals or dedicated research periods, professors can apply their core strengths—hypothesis generation, model design, empirical validation—directly to the development of systematic trading strategies. What makes this possible now is the rise of large language models (LLMs), which dramatically reduce the engineering bottlenecks that once slowed strategy development. Ideas that previously required weeks of software infrastructure can now be implemented and tested in days.

By combining faculty insight with platform support and LLM acceleration, this initiative creates a reliable, scalable funding channel for academic research. Professors contribute directly to strategy design and refinement, and when those strategies succeed, a share of the resulting profits is routed—under transparent university policies—to their research labs or project accounts.

## Why This Moment Is Different

Quantitative strategy development requires two skills: rigorous mathematical research and engineering that turns ideas into robust, testable systems. The first requirement remains essential. What has changed is the time and effort required for the second.

Modern large language models (LLMs) substantially reduce the coding bottleneck. With a reasonable programming background, a researcher can turn a well‑specified idea into high‑quality, maintainable code in days rather than weeks. The scaffolding that once consumed entire cycles—data preparation, backtesting infrastructure, diagnostics—now arrives much faster, allowing the work to center on modeling choices, hypothesis testing, and empirical validation.

This shift makes university professors the ideal leaders for quantitative research in this environment. The core loop of systematic strategy development mirrors academic practice: discover tell‑tale patterns in data, formalize and refine hypotheses, build models, test them, and iterate. Because the translation from idea to implementation is now so much faster, more of the calendar can be devoted to the intellectual heart of the work, and more iterations can fit into a semester, a quarter, or a sabbatical year. As a result, this is an especially opportune moment to establish a platform where professors can spend focused time developing or improving strategies and be compensated accordingly—creating a reliable, alternative funding stream for their research groups within the university.

## What the Program Is

This program invites professors to enhance existing systematic trading strategies or to invent new ones. It provides all essentials — trading capital, curated data, and a managed execution pipeline — so faculty can begin meaningful work immediately and collaborate with peers across universities.

Engagements typically last six to twelve months, organized in quarterly cycles. Most participants begin by strengthening live strategies — refining signals, improving models, or testing scalability — and propose new strategies once the evidence supports it. Work is conducted in small research groups.

Compensation is tied directly to measurable contributions. Ownership is recorded as work progresses, and results are reviewed quarterly. When strategies generate profits, performance compensation is paid according to recorded ownership. At the professor’s discretion — and in line with university policy — payments may be directed to a lab or project account as research funding. A safety net ensures reliable support for active professors even if a strategy stops working — for example, due to market regime changes or shifts in asset rules — by guaranteeing compensation until a defined cumulative threshold (currently set at $1 million) is reached. This allows professors and research groups to treat the platform as a dependable alternative funding source.

The platform manages infrastructure and continuity. It maintains backtesting and evaluation systems, version control, deployment pipelines, and monitoring frameworks, ensuring strategies remain operational as data providers, APIs, or libraries evolve — without imposing maintenance burdens on the professors who created or improved the trading strategies.

For universities, the structure aligns naturally with endowment goals. Trading capital can be sourced from the endowment itself; the professor’s share is distributed (or credited to the lab) under transparent policies, while the remaining profits accrue back to the endowment.

Crucially, the platform creates a self-reinforcing cycle: strong returns attract more capital, which in turn amplifies the impact of promising strategies developed by university professors. As more top-ranked faculty join the platform full-time for dedicated research cycles, the likelihood of discovering robust, high-performing strategies increases. We provide the initial set of trading strategies to kick off the cycle — ensuring momentum from the start.

## Funding and Compensation Model

Professors are compensated directly based on their contributions to strategies that generate profits. Each quarter, 20% of all strategy profits is allocated as a performance pool reserved exclusively for participating professors. This mirrors the industry‑standard “2 and 20” hedge fund model, where 2% covers operational costs and 20% of profits are set aside as performance‑based incentives. After applying a small quarterly management fee (currently 0.25%) to cover platform operations, the remainder of profits stays in the fund to compound.

Within the performance pool, 80% is distributed to professors whose strategies were traded that quarter. Compensation is pro‑rated according to recorded ownership, which tracks who contributed to the strategy and when. When a new strategy is developed, ownership begins as an equal split among its inventors. If another group later improves the strategy—by increasing returns, expanding capacity, or enhancing robustness—and the improvement is verified and accepted, they are added to the ownership group. At that point, shares reset equally across the full team, and all members participate in future decisions about further changes.

The remaining 20% of the performance pool—equivalent to 4% of total profits under current settings—funds a standing safety net for active professors. This ensures predictable, baseline support for those whose cumulative compensation has not yet reached a defined threshold (currently $1 million). The safety net continues until the threshold is met, protecting contributors from adverse market shifts or strategy decay. In line with university policies, compensation is routed to approved research labs or project accounts—making this a transparent, auditable, and mission‑aligned alternative research funding channel.

The model is designed to scale with success. As strategies deliver strong returns, the fund grows through both reinvested profits and new capital inflows. That growth allows the platform to attract more professors and increases the likelihood of consistently discovering robust, high‑performing strategies, which in turn helps to scale the assets under management of the fund. We seed the platform with the initial set of trading strategies to start this cycle and establish early performance benchmarks.

The current parameters—20% performance allocation, 20% of that to the safety net, and a $1M safety net threshold—are reasonable, adjustable policy choices provided here for illustrative simulation purposes. Under conservative modeling with those parameters, approximately 288 professors receive long‑term support, translating to 288 research groups receiving sustained research funding. Five‑year cumulative compensation per research group is substantial: 75th percentile outcomes exceed $850k, and the 90th percentile approaches $2M.

## What the Simulations Indicate

We have run multi‑year, multi‑path simulations to understand the dynamics of funding and compensation under a range of market conditions. The outputs include the evolution of a $100 reference investment (net of all fees and distributions), the distribution of author earnings at five‑year anniversaries, total author counts over time, and the behavior of assets under management under both typical markets and crisis years. Because the net asset value now reflects the full profit and loss each quarter, the paths you see are faithful to how a real fund behaves: losses reduce NAV immediately; recoveries rebuild it even before incentives resume; distributions occur only when policy allows.

For administrators, these simulations translate into planning tools. They show what ranges of support are plausible for research groups under conservative, base‑case, and favorable conditions. For professors, they provide realistic expectations about how contributions convert into ownership, how ownership becomes compensation, and how the safety‑net smooths outcomes early in the lifecycle.
